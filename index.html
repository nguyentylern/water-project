Water

Water is an important nutrient to human health; it helps your body remain stable and remove waste[1]. Looking towards the future to preserve human health, water must remain. As a result, conserving water is important because it allows for its continued use. Conservation also has other effects: by using less water, it can save money, help the environment, and lower energy demand[2].

Additionally, water treatment is important because it allows for the safe consumption of water. Water treatment is a highly involved process, utilizing chemicals like chlorine, iron, and salts and other materials like gravel and filter membranes[3]. Without treatment, water might be contaminated with objects, dust, bacteria, and viruses that can be harmful when consumed or can cause disease.

In Central Valley

Due to corporate water abuse and the over pumping of water wells for agricultural purposes, Central Valley, California is running out of water. Central Valley is most vulnerable to droughts and water scarcity as it’s California’s leading crop producer, producing over 250 different crops with an estimated value of $17 billion per year[4].

However, the profits that agriculture brings to Central Valley has its negatives; the increase in industrial production means an increase water usage for farming and a decrease in water for surrounding communities. The reliance on ground water from the San Joaquin Basin (which helps supply 20% of the nation's groundwater needs), for agriculture puts an extreme amount of demand on the aquifer[5]. During the 2011-2017 California drought, an estimated 12,000 residents were left without water[6]. In addition, gross revenue losses in food processing sectors for the Central Valley were $2.4 billion in 2021 and $3.5 billion in 2022[7]. The lack of water availability has also lead to farmer job loss due to their dependence on water.

Conservation

Learning how to conserve water is a necessary step in ending the decade long drought in Central Valley. Water conservation is using water in the most efficient fashion to reduce water waste. For outdoor water conservation, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) recommends, when landscaping, choosing native plants or plants that need less water, maintaining healthy soils, being selective when adding turf areas, and providing regular maintenance[8]. Xeriscaping, another form of landscaping, also conserves water because of its emphasis on the different selections of plants necessary for water conservation. Using xeriscaping reduces water usage and maintenance costs by over 60%[9]. The California government recommends for indoor water conservation to fix leaky faucets, taking more water efficient showers and less baths, and tracking water bills to check where water usage can be lessened[10]. Alongside this, compost bins are suggested instead of garbage disposal and using water that would be otherwise pour down a drain to water plants is encouraged. Community engagement is also important in raising awareness about the importance of water conservation. Collaborating with local communities to enforce water usage restrictions during drought periods ensures collective effort in protecting water.
